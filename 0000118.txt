Another recession looms as interest rates soar toward record levels. While the slide may not be so deep as last spring's, many economists say, it is expected to be longer, lasting until summer.  Layoffs and production cutbacks in the housing and auto industries are expected. (Page A1.)
 
 Consumer loan costs should remain relatively stable at the new higher levels - 17 percent on car loans, for example - despite the volatility of corporate prime rates, bankers say. A 30-month installment loan should be profitable over its lifetime, they add.  (A1.) Companies
 
 Chrysler is expected to seek $200 million more in loans backed by the Government, and approval is likely. But the auto maker can expect tough going in Washington should it need addi tional Federal backing after the new , Republican-controlled Senate and loan board are in power. (D1.)
 
 Invsco is diversifying beyond condominium conversions. The company, which is the nation's leader in converting rental apartments and has been the target of Congressional pressure, is moving into construction and seeking takeovers. (D1.)
 
 United Technologies ponders its course should its president, Alexander M. Haig Jr., become Secretary of State in the Reagan administration. He is described as the President-elect's choice.  (D1.)International
 
 Saudi Arabia will support an oil price freeze next Monday when OPEC meets in Indonesia, Oil Minister Sheikh Ahmed Zaki Yamani said. Other ministers said the meeting would be held despite the war between Iran and Iraq, both member states. (D1.)
 
 The rush of foreign banks to Luxembourg has slowed to a trickle, largely because of the worldwide recession and its squeeze on banking profits. Some 112 are ensconsed in the Grand Duchy. (D1.)
 
 Brazil, the biggest debtor in the third world, is under pressure from lenders of the $56 billion it owes to restrict its economic growth through reduced credit expansion and increased currency devaluations. Citibank, Chase Manhattan and Bank of America lead a long list of creditor institutions. (D1.) Today's Columns
 
 Administration moves to curb Japanese car imports last summer were dashed when the Justice Department warned the ''voluntary'' limits might violate antitrust laws. Washington Watch. (D2.)
 
 Distress selling of cattle has led to lower retail beef prices. But the pork and bacon prices are still relatively high, jolting the futures markets. Commodities. (D8.)

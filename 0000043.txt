Retailers felt little Christmas cheer on the biggest shopping weekend of the year, despite crowded stores and shopping centers during the last weekend before Christmas. Many customers were moving from store to store shopping for bargains. Shoppers and merchants cite inflation, fears of soaring credit costs and general economic uncertainty. (Page D1.)
 
 Gift shoppers are favoring gold jewelry and bullion coins this Christmas season, according to retailers. The emphasis is on the higher-karat jewelry and smaller denomination of Krugerrands.  Merchants say sales are slower in gold-plated items. (D1.) Companies
 
 Chrysler is struggling to keep its net worth from falling below zero, at which point the auto maker technically would be owned by its creditors. While zero net worth does not mean the company would be bankrupt, it could imperil Chrysler's chances of drawing more of the $1.5 billion in Federal loan guarantees authorized it. (D1.)
 
 Chemical companies brace for stiffer costs of disposing of dangerous waste materials, which may involve some 57 million tons this year. The new and tougher laws imposed by the Environmental Protectection Agency last month are supported by a $1.6 billion Federal ''superfund'' to aid in this work. (D1.) International
 
 Japan's Cabinet has approved its most austere budget in 22 years, despite the Government's official view that 1981 will be another period of economic growth. The proposed $223.8 billion budget also envisions reducing the size of Government bond issues. (D1.)
 
 Quebec's shipbuilding industry will complete only one vessel this year, but its yards are booming with orders for railway cars, oil rigs and other nonmarine industrial equipment. (D3.)
 
 South Korea, Asia's biggest borrower, expects next year's debt in foreign money markets to approximate this year's $8.6 billion.  However, South Korea's fiscal officials hope to reduce the 45 percent inflation rate by half in 1981, and hold its trade deficits to just above this year's estimated $5.3 billion.

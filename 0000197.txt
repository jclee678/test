The new state banking law approved by New York legislators could result in a buying surge if individuals take advantage of current interest rates on unused credit lines. The measure, which Governor Carey is likely to sign by tomorrow, is expected to result in sharply higher consumer interest rates. (Page A1.)
 
 The securities industry is prospering as stock market volume soars. Despite the uncertain economic outlook in the U.S., many brokerage houses expect record profits and record returns on capital for 1980.  A good deal of the recent surge has apparently come from institutional investors. (D1.)
 
 New machine tool orders plunged 42 percent last month from the October 1979 level. It was the third consecutive month in which this key economic indicator has fallen. But the backlog of orders at the end of October still represented 14 months' production. (D1.)
 
 Institutions are investing more in farmland, once considered risky by insurance companies and pension funds. Higher inflation and lower-yielding traditional investments are luring the institutions.  But some worry that the flow of large sums into the market will cause economic distortions. (D1.)
 
 Washington is being urged to use its food exporting power to persuade importing lands to establish grain reserves, rather than depend on uninterrupted supplies from this country. An American advisory group to the United Nations has also suggested that the U.S. not lift its grain embargo against the Soviet Union without asking Moscow's aid in helping to stabilize world food supplies and prices.  (D7.) Companies
 
 MGM Grand Hotels faces an uncertain financial future following the fire that damaged its Las Vegas resort-casino. Company officials do not expect the complex to reopen until July 1. (D1.)
 
 Ashland Oil is awaiting Saudi Arabia's decision on a proposal to build a $500 million plant that would mak e lubricating oils. The plant, on the Red Sea, would be a joint venture with the Saudi stateowned oi l company, Petromin. (D5.)
 
 Steelmakers, enjoying a large increase in fourth-quarter orders, are still cautious about continued business. Some say current orders may reflect hedge buying before new higher prices take effect. (D1.) Today's Columns
 
 The Reagan camp is divided over whether to allow the Office of the U.S. Trade Representative to negotiate treaties or just set policy.  Washington Watch. (D2.)
 
 Scrap copper and ''exotic'' metals prices continue to lag despite the sharp increases that are expected in defense spending under the Reagan administration. Commodities. (D7.)

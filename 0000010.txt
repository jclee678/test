Curtiss-Wright, to thwart a hostile takeover by Kennecott, offered $44 a share for one million of its own shares and said it was seeking funds to purchase 2.1 million more, or 37 1/2 percent in all, at the same price. Wall Street professional traders said privately that they would accept Kennecott's $40-a-share offer, which expired at midnight last night. (Page 27.)
 
 Allegheny Ludlum Steel was sold for $195 million to a group of investors, including top executives of the company and George W.  Tippins, a Pittsburgh industrialist. Clint W. Murchison Jr., owner of the Dallas Cowboys, who had also bid for the nation's largest stainless steel producer, said he understood the desire to sell it to a local group and would not ''make a fuss.'' (27.)
 
 Mitsubishi Motors said it would set up an American marketing subsidiary to avoid legal complications in the event of a Chrysler bankruptcy. Mitsubishi, which makes Dodge Colts and Plymouth Champs for Chrysler, wants to be sure it can retain ownership of any cars Chrysler has ordered but not yet paid for. (28.) International
 
 Britain's monetarist policies are destroying the industrial base, many fear, with unemployment at 9 percent and inflation at 15 1/2 percent. But Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher and the Conservative economists around her argue that with perseverance Government efforts to control the money supply and public spending will work. The Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development forecasts that unemployment will rise to 12 percent by mid-1982, while the gross national product, now declining at a 2 1/4 percent annual rate, will move up only to zero growth. (1.)
 
 Arab oil-producers will continue to invest the bulk of their surplus funds in the United States and in American banks, according to a report made public by Chase World Information Corporation, despite the freezing of Iranian assets by President Carter. The size of the American economy, its stability and the large number of investment possibilities will draw in the funds, the report said.  (29.)
 
 American payments for imported natural gas could rise by $3.5 billion annually if the United States agreed to an Algerian demand for a 200 percent increase in the price of its liquefied natural gas, a study by the General Accounting Office said. It concluded that ''importing large amounts of L.N.G. from OPEC countries is not in the national interest.'' Algeria halted shipments in April after the Carter Administration refused to accept Algeria's demand. (30.) Markets
 
 Defense, computer and semiconductor stocks gained smartly as the market edged up in the lightest trading in more than two years.  Volume was 16.1 million shares. The Dow Jones industrial average gained 3.33 points, to 966.38. The Dow rose 29 points in Christmas week trading, spurred by the Wells Fargo Bank's cut in the prime rate on Monday, to 20 1/2 percent. Boeing, the most active issue, climbed 1 1/2, to 42 1/4. (27.) The dollar gained marginally against all major currencies except the pound and the Swiss franc in light, pos@tholiday trading. The pound rose to $2.3810 in New York from $2.3785 on Wednesday. Major gold trading centers were closed. (34.) The Economy
 
 ''Creative financing'' is being used to spur home sales as mortgage rates climb, realtors report. Among the methods adopted are financing by the seller or builder, leases with an option to buy, trades of property and contracts for deeds. (27.)
 
 Many New York merchants are being forced to relocate or close, particularly on the upper West Side, because of tremendous rent increases. Brooks TV and Radio at Broadway and 81st said it was forced out of business by a rise to $10,000 a month from $1,300.  (23.)
 
 New York City business bank loans rose by $358 million, to $51.3 billion, in the week ended Dec. 17, the Federal Reserve reported. A third of the loans went to foreign businesses. Borrowing in the commercial paper market, however, declined by $888 million. (35.) Today's Columns
 
 Social Security taxes will rise once again on New Year's Day. The burden will be greatest on those making $25,000 to $30,000 a year.  They would need a salary rise of several hundred dollars a year just to pay the tax. Your Money. (28.)
 
 A NICE way to store winter cold has been patented by an engineering professor from George Washington University. Shao W. Yuan uses the acronym for his Natural Ice for Cooling Energy, which utilizes an underground storage tank. Patents. (30.)

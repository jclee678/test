The nation's larger banks raised their prime rates t o 19 percent from 18 1/2 percent, increasing the likelihoo d of a recession next year. The rate rise came in response to the F ederal Reserve's stringent monetary policy and it brought the prime to its highest level since April, when it reached a record 2 0 percent. Bankers and economists predicted, cautiously in view of m ixed economic indicators, that the prime might exceed that peak if the Federal Reserve extends its present tight-money polic ies. (Page 1.)
 
 The nation's money supply increased $1.2 billion in the last week of November, the Federal Reserve reported. The rise surprised many economists, who had expected a decline. They predicted record interest rates would result. (29.)
 
 The jobless rate slipped by one-tenth of a percentage point in November - to 7.5 percent - in what economists see as evidence of continuing, though spotty, economic recovery. (9.)
 
 Producer prices rose broadly in November, but at a slower pace than in October, the Labor Department said. Food price declines helped slow the rise in the overall index of finished goods to six-tenths of 1 percent from eight-tenths of 1 percent in October. Analysts expect 1 percent climbs in consumer prices in November and December.  (29.)
 
 Retail sales in New York City rose 7.4 percent in November, compared to level a year earlier - barely keeping pace with the rate of the stores' price increases - a monthly spot-check found. (31.) Markets
 
 The Dow Jones industrial average plunged 14.25 points, to close at 956.23, and Big Board volume expanded to 52 million shares. Declines outnumbered advances by a ratio of more than 3 to 1, with energy issues particularly hard-hit. The Dow average was off 37.11 points for the week, as investors continued to worry about rising interest rates. (29.) The dollar rose against most foreign currencies. Gold prices fell to $614.25 in London from $630 the day before, but recovered a bit in later New York trading. Republic National quoted gold at $620, down $2. (32.) In commodity trading, oil and sugar futures prices fell, while cocoa rose a bit.  A report that the Soviet Union had found the largest oil field ever was ridiculed by industry and Government experts. But after it was carried on news wires, nervous investors began selling oil stocks.  The field was said to total 619 billion tons, more than twice the world's estimated recoverable reserves. ''It's an absurdity, an impossibility,'' said Robert LeVine, an analyst for E.F. Hutton.  (30.)
 
 China's huge cultural and trade exposition opened at the New York Coliseum, beginning the final leg of a coast-to-coast journey. It is the largest such display ever to leave China. (29.) Companies
 
 Arrow Electronics Inc. and its newly appointed chairman are struggling to cope with both personal and professional loss in the aftermath of the tragic hotel fire that killed many of its executives, including two of the three officers who founded the company in its modern form. (29.)
 
 Volkswagen said its profits fell to $18.5 million in the third quarter, bringing the nine-month total to $129.8 million, down 42.2 percent from a year earlier. It cited the poor performance of its overseas subsidiaries, notably those in the United States and Brazil. The announcement came two days after VW disclosed plans to cooperate with Nissan to build vehicles in Japan. (31.)
 
 United Telecommunications said it had agreed to sell its Calma subsidiary, which develops and markets computer-aided design and manufacturing systems, to General Electric for $100 million. (31.) Today's Columns

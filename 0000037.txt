Chrysler's plea for a wage and benefits freeze was rejected by the Chrysler Council of the United Automobile Workers union. The group said, however, that it would renegotiate its contract with the auto maker, which is trying to cut costs and improve its financial condition. Chrysler is asking its workers to forgo $600 million in wages and benefits as part of a $1.5 cost-savings plan. (Page A1.)
 
 Curtiss-Wright, fighting a $335 million takeover by Kennecott, announced that Tamco Enterprises had offered to swap its stock in City Investing and $58 million in cash for Curtiss-Wright's stake in Kennecott. Kennecott expressed opposition to the offer. (D1.)
 
 May Department Stores and Prudential Insurance said they have agreed to form a partnership to own and operate six regional shopping centers now operated by a May subsidiary. (D11.) Markets
 
 The Dow Jones industrial average surged 21.59 points, to 958.79, largely because of a surprise cut in the prime rate by Wells Fargo Bank. Blue-chip issues led the advance. (D1.) The Treasury auctioned new six-month bills at 14.03 percent, a steep decline from the rate set at last week's sale. Other short and long term rates also moved lower. (D5.)
 
 The dollar dropped sharply on currency markets in the face of falling interest rates. Gold declined $2.10 an ounce in New York but rose $18 an ounce in Zurich, where it closed at $593.50.(D12.) Grain futures prices closed mixed in light pre-holiday activity, with wheat lower and corn and soybeans higher. (D12.) International
 
 Unemployment in industrial nations will increase during the next 18 months, though the current recession will begin to fade in the next six months, according to the semi-annual economic forecast of the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development. (D1.)
 
 Japan will spend far less for defense next year than the United States had hoped. Japan's draft budget for the fiscal year 1981 calls for a 6.6 percent increase in defense spending, to $11.5 billion. A United States official had said 9.7 percent was the minimum desirable. (A9.)
 
 World airlines lost an estimated $2.5 billion this year in their worst year ever, an official of the International Air Transport Association said. Rising fuel prices and inflation pushed operating costs up 27 percent; revenues rose only 19.2 percent. (D4.) The Economy
 
 Americans increased their spending and cut their savings last month as personal incomes kept slightly ahead of inflation, the Commerce Department said. It said personal income rose $24.9 billion, or 1.1 percent in November, while personal spending jumped $21.3 billion, or 1.2 percent. (D1.)
 
 Exploration for oil and gas deposits in Georges Bank will be allowed to proceed in return for a greater Government commitment to protect the rich fishing grounds, under a settlement the Federal Government reached with the State of Massachusetts and fishermen and conservation groups. The government has already sold drilling leases for 20,000 square miles off the Massachusetts coast. (A1.)
 
 Commercial bankers and municipal government officials are challenging a ruling by the Internal Revenue Service that would raise the cost of money to state and municipal governments and subject banks to millions of dollars of liability for back taxes.  (D2.)
 
 The ceiling on the royalties record companies pay songwriters and music publishers will be increased to 4 cents per song from 2 3/4 cents, effective next July. The Recording Industry Association of America said it was appealing the decision by the Copyright Royalty Tribunal. (D11.) Today's Columns
 
 Andrew S. Grove, president of Intel, said that the casual atmosphere at his company results from its concentration on essence rather than form. Though the people coming in the door tend to be timid and inexperienced, he remarked, they must still be forced to decide things like the form of the next microcomputers. Talking Business. (D2.)
 
 A sharp rise in the price of the stock of Cooper Industries once again made its bid for the shares of Crouse-Hinds competitive with a rival bid from Internorth. The Cooper share rise was attributed to heavy buying by a Texas money manager. Market Place. (D6.)

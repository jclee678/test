The prime lending rate was raised to 17 3/4 percent, from 17 percent by dozens of the nation's largest banks. The move was the fourth since the Presidential election earlier this month, when the rate stood at 14 1/2 percent. Bankers attributed the rise to their own higher costs for funds and warned that the higher cost of borrowing could bring about another recession. Meanwhile, Wells Fargo, the nation's 11th largest bank increased its prime rate a full percentage point, to 18 percent. (Page A1.)
 
 Business productivity rose slightly less than previously estimated in the third quarter, the Labor Department reported. The Department said that the productivity of all private business rose at an annual rate of 0.3 percent, down from its October estimate of 1.4 percent, an indication that inflation may be worsening. (D2.)
 
 Deposits at savings and loan associations picked up substantially in October, as customers were lured by fast-rising interest rates on popular six-month money market certificates, the Federal Home Loan Bank Board said.  A bond trader can be charged with fraud for violating a duty to give his employer information necessary for conducting the employer's business, a Federal appeals court ruled in New York. (D3.) Energy
 
 In a move expected to exert upward pressure on oil prices, Indonesia is raising the premiums on its spot oil prices by $1 to $1.70 a barrel effective Dec. 1, according to oil industry sources.  But analysts said the spot market generally was softening. In addition, a Caracas newspaper quoted Energy Minister Humberto Calderon Berti as saying that Venezuela would again raise prices for residual fuel oil, which would lift heating bills in the Eastern United States. (D1.)
 
 The nation's small refiners are gearing up for what is expected to be a struggle for survival. The remaining Federal programs for pricecontrols and allocations are to expire in September. (D4.)
 
 The West German Government is concerned about possible opposition by President-elect Ronald Reagan to a proposed pipeline project that would increase to 30 percent from 15 percent West Germany's dependence on the Soviet Union for natural gas. (D4.)

 Carl H. Lindner is seeking to take his American Financial Corporation private. The board of American Financial, which the astute but publicity-shy Cincinnati financier has built into a $3 billion enterprise, said it was considering the proposal. The proposal involves a merger whereby the two brothers and their families would own all of American Financial's common stock. (D1.)
